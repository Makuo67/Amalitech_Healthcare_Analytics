Design Decision

Decision 1: Fact Table Grain

Option A — One row per encounter

Justification

A grain of one row per encounter is the most optimal for the four analytical queries in this project. This level of granularity allows us to:

Aggregate metrics at the encounter, provider, specialty, and department level.
Calculate readmissions, encounter counts, unique patients, and financial metrics efficiently.
Avoid generating extremely large fact tables (as would happen in diagnosis-level or procedure-level grains).
Support joins to other dimensions using surrogate keys without duplicating rows.

A diagnosis-level or procedure-level grain would unnecessarily expand the fact table and slow down queries that are encounter-centered.

provider_last_name
credential

Note: In a star schema, specialty & department are separate dimensions, not columns inside provider.



Decision 3: Pre-Aggregated Metrics

The encounter-level fact table will store metrics that are expensive to compute repeatedly from the OLTP system.

Metrics to store:
- diagnosis_count

Number of diagnoses associated with this encounter.

- procedure_count

Number of procedures associated with this encounter.

- total_claim_amount

Sum of all claim_amount values for that encounter.

- total_allowed_amount

Sum of all allowed_amount values for that encounter.

- has_readmission (0/1)

Indicates whether a readmission occurred within 30 days.

- length_of_stay (in days)

Computed from discharge_date and encounter_date.

Why Pre-Aggregation Helps Performance
Reduces the need for complex multi-join operations.
Avoids repeatedly scanning the encounter_diagnoses and encounter_procedures tables.
Eliminates COUNT(DISTINCT) operations, which are slow.
Allows BI queries to operate on a single clean fact table.
Moves computation from query-time to ETL-time, improving dashboard performance dramatically.
Decision 4: Bridge Tables
- Yes — Use bridge tables for many-to-many relationships.

In healthcare, diagnoses and procedures have true many-to-many relationships with encounters:

One encounter may have multiple diagnoses.
One encounter may have multiple procedures.
This cannot be handled efficiently in a single fact table without row explosion.
Bridge Tables to include:
fact_encounter_diagnosis_bridge
encounter_key
diagnosis_key
diagnosis_sequence
fact_encounter_procedure_bridge
encounter_key
procedure_key
procedure_date

Decision 4
Why bridge tables are needed
Prevents the fact table grain from becoming “per diagnosis” or “per procedure.”
Avoids large Cartesian explosion during joins.
Supports detailed clinical analyses without duplicating fact table rows.
Gives flexibility to analysts querying diagnoses or procedures separately.

Without bridge tables, diagnosis × procedure combinations can explode into millions of rows per query.
